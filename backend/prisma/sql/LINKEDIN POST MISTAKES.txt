His posts are solid and informed, but they read like mini-lectures instead of conversations, and they lack emotional hooks, clear positioning, and community feel—so people don’t feel compelled to react or comment.
​

Patterns in his content
Topics are generic B2B marketing: ads planning, landing pages, cold email dying, onboarding, workflows—useful, but broad and not clearly tied to a narrow, specific type of founder or industry.
​

All posts are advice-style monologues: they explain what “most people” do wrong and what “smart marketers” do, but rarely include his own stories, failures, or client examples to humanize the advice.
​

Almost no personal stakes: there’s no “this happened to me last week” or “here’s what I did with a client,” so readers don’t build a relationship with him as a person.
​

Writing style and structure issues
Same rhythm in every post: short punchy lines, but all in the same “Most people do X. Smart people do Y. Do Y or you’re toast.” pattern, which starts to feel repetitive and preachy.
​

Tone is a bit scolding: phrases like “you’re wasting money,” “you’re toast,” “you’re just spinning your wheels,” and “if you’re still winging it, you’re leaving money on the table” can trigger defensiveness instead of connection.
​

No emotional hook in line 1: the first lines are often statements (“Most people run ads without a real plan.” “Most onboarding processes miss the real bottleneck.”), not curiosity-driving or emotional hooks, so people scroll past.
​

Engagement mechanics
Weak or late calls to action: only one post explicitly says “Like this post if you agree… Comment with your biggest workflow challenge,” and it’s attached at the end of a long, abstract explanation.
​

Questions are broad and effortful: asking for someone’s “biggest workflow challenge” requires thinking and vulnerability; there are no lightweight questions like “Have you ever…?” or simple yes/no prompts.
​

No community identity: he speaks to “most teams,” “most campaigns,” “your growth,” but doesn’t give followers a name or identity (like “founder-operators,” “pipeline obsessives,” etc.), so it feels one-to-many, not community-based.
​

Positioning and relevance
Headline is strong but not reflected in posts: his profile says he helps founders go from validation to profitability and stops unpredictable marketing “1 lead at a time,” yet posts often sound like generic agency/CMO thought leadership, not stories about that journey.
​

Little specificity in audience language: posts don’t call out specific types of founders (SaaS, agencies, local, etc.), business stages, or concrete scenarios, so they feel like they could be for anyone—and end up resonating with no one.
​

No proof or mini case studies: he doesn’t show “here’s what we did, here’s the before/after,” so people don’t see evidence, only theory.
​

Why this leads to low engagement (with concrete fixes)
Feels like being taught, not talked with: the “most people do it wrong” framing puts the reader on the defensive; switch some posts to “Here’s what I messed up and learned,” then ask “Have you done this too?” to invite vulnerability.
​

High cognitive load, low emotional payoff: posts ask readers to process concepts (workflows, onboarding audits, tracking) without tying them to emotional stakes (stress, wasted budget, fear of being fired); add “this is what keeps founders awake at 2am” type framing.
​

No habit-forming series: there’s no recurring series name or format (e.g., “1 Lead at a Time #01”) that gives people a reason to come back; creating a series with episodic stories could build familiarity.
